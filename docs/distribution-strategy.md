# Trust-first distribution and funnel plan

> **Status:** working strategy, not editorial doctrine.
> [THESIS.md](../THESIS.md), [METHOD.md](../METHOD.md), and
> [EDITORIAL.md](../EDITORIAL.md) govern when this document disagrees with them.

Bitter Frontier should grow by becoming unusually useful to the people who
choose, integrate, govern, and operate coding agents. Distribution begins after
the reporting earns attention. It cannot repair an ordinary article, and it
must never weaken the receipt standard to buy reach.

The funnel has four jobs:

1. Help a new reader discover one useful idea or reported change.
2. Prove the publication's judgment through primary receipts and comparative
   synthesis.
3. Give the reader a reason to return before the next urgent decision.
4. Offer Bitter as a disclosed continuation of the argument, outside the
   analysis and only after Frontier has delivered value on its own.

Trust first, product second.

## What travels

Frontier has three complementary distribution units:

- **The reported case:** a concrete change, the channel an operator can run,
  and the consequence. This is the strongest unit for timely social posts,
  communities, and citations.
- **The cross-project read:** a precedent or divergence that changes what the
  event means. This is the publication's most distinctive weekly contribution.
- **The durable explanation:** concept pages and dated project profiles that
  answer recurring questions from search, internal links, and AI assistants.

The Bitter Lesson and Amdahl's law pages are the center of an internal-linking
system, not slogans pasted onto every article. A report should link to a concept
when the concept clarifies the case. Each concept page should link back to
reporting that tests it, including evidence that complicates the thesis.

## The funnel by reader need

### Discovery

Meet readers where they already have a question.

- Write search pages around durable questions, then keep their claims and
  modification dates honest. Do not refresh dates without material changes.
- Publish a small number of human-written, channel-native excerpts from each
  strong issue. One should carry the reported case; another may carry the
  synthesis. Both link to the canonical article.
- Share a report in a technical community only when it directly answers the
  discussion already happening there. Participate as an informed editor, not
  as a link account.
- Make precise excerpts, stable URLs, metadata, and primary receipts easy for
  other writers and assistants to cite.

Frontier does not need a post on every network for every issue. The editor
chooses the channel where the specific argument is native and useful.

### Proof

The destination must immediately justify the click.

- Lead with the case and operator consequence, not publication doctrine.
- Put claim-bearing links on the words they prove.
- Show release status and uncertainty without making the reader learn the
  research pipeline.
- Link related signals, profiles, and concept pages where they shorten the
  reader's path to understanding.
- Keep Bitter out of the analysis. A publisher note or next step must be
  visually and editorially separate.

The best conversion asset is a page worth sending to a colleague who will never
buy the product.

### Return

A busy operator needs a low-attention way to stay current.

- Keep RSS complete and dependable.
- Add email only when there is a clear consent, delivery, and archive workflow.
  The email should be a concise edition of the canonical article, not a second
  publication with different claims.
- Let readers follow the smallest useful unit where practical, such as all
  digests or a project profile, without manufacturing notifications for
  immaterial changes.
- Use the weekly cadence as a promise of judgment, not a quota. A thin research
  window should not become a padded issue.

### Company handoff

Readers who agree with the diagnosis may want the operating environment that
follows from it. The handoff should be explicit and restrained.

- Identify Bitter as the publisher everywhere disclosure matters.
- At the end of relevant concept or publication pages, offer one clear link to
  the product outside the reported argument.
- Explain the continuity: Frontier tracks where general agents improve and
  where human attention becomes serial; Bitter is designed around those same
  constraints.
- Do not gate receipts, punish readers who decline, or turn provider findings
  into disguised product comparisons.

If the article stops being useful without the product link, the funnel has
failed.

The SheetGenius portfolio card is part of this handoff and should use the same
positioning as the publication hero. Recommended copy:

> Source-backed reporting on what coding agents made cheaper, where the
> bottleneck moved, and what deserves scarce human attention.

Do not leave the card on the pre-pivot "weekly field notes on what broke" copy.
That framing makes Frontier sound like a release-note monitor and hides the
comparative Bitter Lesson and Amdahl synthesis that now defines it.

## Publish once, derive carefully

The Frontier site is canonical. Every external version should point back to it
and preserve the meaning, receipts, uncertainty, and release status of the
published article.

A publish packet may contain:

1. one plain-language sentence stating what changed;
2. one sentence stating who should act and how;
3. one cross-project observation, when comparison is earned;
4. the canonical URL and a useful image or excerpt;
5. platform-specific drafts for editorial approval.

Automation may prepare drafts. A human editor chooses whether the article is
worth distributing, which claim can stand alone, and whether the destination
still supports it. No outbound system may publish unverified research
candidates.

Long-form syndication is optional. Use it only when the destination preserves a
canonical link, readable receipts, and correction behavior. A partial excerpt
that sends the reader to the source is often simpler and safer.

## Search and authority

Search growth should be a consequence of a coherent body of useful work.

- Give each evergreen page one clear reader question and a descriptive title.
- Link the first substantive use of Bitter Lesson Maxing and Amdahl Maxing to
  their canonical explainers.
- Link concept pages to concrete reports where the lens changed the conclusion.
- Link reports to dated profiles for current posture, while preserving the
  report's original window truth.
- Keep author, published, modified, and source information accurate.
- Make corrections visible. Authority compounds when the record can be audited,
  including when Frontier changes its mind.

Avoid pages created only to capture near-duplicate keywords. The publication's
advantage is original synthesis, not surface area.

## Earned distribution

The highest-quality external attention comes from other people using Frontier
as evidence.

- Give specialist writers, newsletter editors, podcast hosts, and technical
  communities a precise case they can verify, not a generic publication pitch.
- Submit only exceptional, self-contained work to aggregators. Do not coordinate
  votes or repeat-submit routine editions.
- Invite outside experts to challenge a narrow claim when their expertise can
  materially improve it. Their participation is editorial work first and
  distribution second.
- Treat social posts as leads or cultural context unless they are themselves the
  primary artifact being claimed. They do not replace product documentation,
  code, releases, or reproducible observations.

## What to measure

Measure whether the publication saves and earns scarce human attention:

- qualified readers who return;
- subscriptions and feed follows from people in the intended audience;
- references, backlinks, and citations to specific reporting;
- movement from a concept page into a report, and from a report into related
  analysis;
- voluntary visits from Frontier to Bitter after the reader has consumed useful
  editorial work;
- replies that reveal a changed operator decision, a missed consequence, or a
  credible correction.

Raw impressions and follower counts are diagnostic, not editorial objectives.
No metric outranks the receipt, reader utility, or the right to publish a result
that cuts against Bitter.

## Sequence

### Established in the thesis pivot

1. The canonical thesis, method, editorial standard, founder letter, and two
   concept pages form the public spine.
2. Concept pages link to supporting and complicating reports; published artifact
   pages link back to the explainers.
3. The new pages have stable routes, article metadata, sitemap entries, and
   social cards.

### Now

1. Establish a repeatable, human-approved publish packet for the best issues.
2. Add measurement for movement among concept pages, reporting, return paths,
   and the separated publisher handoff.
3. Build a small set of direct relationships with writers and communities whose
   readers face the same operator decisions.

### Next

1. Add a simple retention path, beginning with the existing feed and then email
   if the operating workflow is ready.
2. Use search and referral data to find unanswered reader questions, then answer
   them only when Frontier can add original, receipted value.

### Later

Consider new formats, data feeds, or syndication only when the existing
publication has evidence of repeat use. Each extension must pass the same
question: does it improve verified reader value per unit of editorial attention,
or does it merely create more output to maintain?
